Dear Dr. Maturilli,

On a recent trip to Svalbard to visit a friend, I was struck by the beauty of the Arctic. This experience made me eager to spend more time in the Arctic, which led me to this position. During my trip, I learned about Arctic logistics through several smaller expeditions to the Fridtjovbreen and Atomfjella areas. These trips required careful planning and extra safety measures due to the harsh conditions in Svalbard. I enjoyed using my avalanche assessment and risk management skills which I developed as a freeride guide and through my hobby, alpinism. Adapting them to the Arctic with its unique dangers proved to be very interesting. The handling of the risks of polar bears and the obtaining of a Sysselmesteren weapons permit was also a fascinating experience.

For the Engineering position at the AWIPEV station, I’m excited to use the knowledge I gained during my studies abroad at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University (ERAU). There, I worked on marine field projects, developed and tested sand penetrometer robots, and learned the importance of reliable and well-documented measurements. This experience was further enhanced by my time in the Research and Development Department at MinCam, where I regularly tested and assessed prototypes.

Having just completed my Master’s in Electrical Systems Engineering, I’m eager to gain practical field experience. I’m particularly motivated by the importance of Arctic research in understanding climate change. The potential negative feedback loops from glacial melt and permafrost degradation due to climate change are pressing issues that need immediate attention. By contributing to this research, I hope to help improve our understanding of these critical issues. I am a strong team player, motivated to work in an international environment, and excited to meet and collaborate with new people.
